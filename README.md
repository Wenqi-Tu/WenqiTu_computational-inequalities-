This project focuses on the issue of computational inequality in public automatic speech recognition systems (ASR), specifically concentrating on the performance of Chinese-accented English during recognition. As more and more sectors such as banking, healthcare and government affairs adopt automatic voice service systems to replace human customer service. Voice recognition technology has become an important entry point for users to access services. However, such systems are usually trained based on standard English speech data, which means that non-native speakers like Chinese international students may encounter higher rates of misrecognition and service failures. When the system fails to correctly recognize the user's voice, the recognition error will continue to magnify throughout the process, resulting in the user being assigned to the wrong service path and unable to obtain the required services. In this process, the cost of communication is transferred from the system to users, who have to adapt to the system by repeating, simplifying sentences and even deliberately imitating "standard pronunciation".  This difference is not just a technical error, but constitutes a structural inequality. 
In the system flow, the automatic speech recognition system is not a single interactive process, but a complex calculation process composed of multiple stages. Voice is first collected and converted into audio signal by the system, and then preprocessed (such as noise filtering and segmentation). The speech is then transcribed into text using an automatic speech recognition model, which is then processed by the natural language understanding module for intent classification. Finally, the system makes a service decision. Once the initial speech transcription is deviated, all subsequent links will be affected, resulting in recognition errors.
This project integrates various types of evidence and analytical methods to explore how inequality gradually emerges in the calculation process. First, by comparing the data samples of Chinese-accened English and standard-native accent, we can directly observe the performance differences between different groups. Second, the whisper speech recognition model is used as an experimental tool, and the word error rate (WER) is used to measure the performance of the model. Third, by comparing the difference between "ground_truth" and "prediction", Calculate the proportions of substitution, deletion and insertion errors to measure the accuracy of the recognition. The analysis results show that the average error rate of Chinese-accented English is significantly higher than that of standard native English, and the stability of the recognition results is also poor. The errors mainly occur in the substitution type, that is, the system tends to identify certain words as same words with similar pronunciations. Although these types of errors are relatively subtle at the word level, they will gradually be magnified in the system process and affect the subsequent intent recognition and service decision-making. By screening out high-error samples (such as WER >0.30), the most severe cases of system failure can be identified, thereby further revealing its structural deviations. Finally, the design is presented through the HTML interface, showing the recognition confidence, uncertain parts, and the judgment process of intentions. This makes the previously invisible system logic understandable to the users, thereby presenting more clearly how inequality is generated within the system.
